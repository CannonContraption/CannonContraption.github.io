1. I Like Fancy Keyboards

This is becoming less and less unusual as time goes on, but I’m a big fan of well-built keyboards. Many people call these "mechanical", though that descriptor is a very loaded term, in my opinion. My experience with keyboards isn’t nearly as broad as with video games, but I feel I have some interesting opinions to share on the topic, so I may as well write about it.

2. What Makes a Keyboard Good?

Good keyboards have a number of advantages, but most of them are pretty subjective. Different types of boards, layouts, interfaces, switch mechanisms, and a variety of other factors determine what makes a good keyboard.

2.1. Board Type

Starting with some of the less significant factors, there’s the type of board. This includes the body, mounting plate, and physical structure of the case. Some boards have the switches entirely exposed, with the switches protruding from an exposed plate, some use a surround to recess the keys, making the unit feel a little bit more integrated. Generally this doesn’t have a ton of bearing on how the board is perceived, but something really cheaply made will make the rest of the board feel cheap. Rigid materials are usually preferred.

2.2. Layouts

This is probably the most subjective part of this entire post. I’m not going to make calls about what is better, but I’ll briefly touch on my own preferences at the end.

Starting from the most obvious, there’s physical board layouts. The standard variety that most PC users are accustomed to is the 104-key PC layout. Even if you use a Mac, the basic layout of your keyboard mirrors the 104-key standard with only minor modifications. However, there are also 10-keyless, which are the same without the number pad, navless, which have no navigation cluster, 60%, which leave out the function row, nav cluster, and number pad, split ergonomic keyboards, which separate the hands by some amount of space, 75%, which rearranged the nav cluster to fit on the side of the board, and leaves out the number pad, and many more. These are just some of the most common ones. I don’t think I’ll probably go into a lot more detail on this later, so if you see me referring to some specific layout, if I don’t describe it you should look to other sources to find out what they are. Chances are good, however, that I’ll just describe what I mean.

Less common is alphanumeric arrangement changes. Most people who know much about keyboards probably know what the Dvorak layout is, at a minimum. Boards with this layout are generally fairly available, as are replacement keycaps for retrofitting a board with Dvorak legends. In my case, I use regular QWERTY boards, remapped in software to use the Workman layout. I think this brings a good balance of efficiency and comfort improvements over QWERTY without requiring as much retraining as Dvorak, or completely new hardware. I have a toggle to go back to QWERTY for in case I need to hand off my computer to someone else for a moment.

2.3. Interfaces

Everyone uses USB these days, but PS/2 is still common. There are specific reasons why someone would opt for one interface or another, but that’s usually for compatibility with exotic hardware. There are some theoretical advantages to PS/2, but I have yet to see any tangible benefit of it on modern systems.

2.4. Switches

This is where I’ll probably spend the most time. This part is the part that most affects the feel of a keyboard. Most common is of course the rubber dome switch. If you own a laptop, if it uses scissor or butterfly switches, these are just stabilizing mechanisms for rubber domes. Next most common would be Cherry MX switches. There are others, but this is really where the bulk of the market is. These generally mount to the board in the same way as each other, but they don’t always share quite the same mechanism. In terms of cross-compatibility, the broadest market for boards, switches, and keycaps is the Cherry MX world. Rubber dome boards are usually made to be cheap to manufacture first, and therefore each board comes with its own designs to mount keycaps and stabilize keys.

Right now, I’m writing this article using Kaihua Kailh Box White switches, which I expect I’ll be reviewing at some point.
